Course Introduction: Bill Nelson 62'

When I was a student at Reed, I lived a reclusive life, mainly in the chemistry building. Folk dancing changed all that, and it has continued to be integral to my life, allowing me to cultivate friends from all around the world.

Folk dancing, first and foremost, is about the joy of moving to music, combined with the celebration of community. The dances are usually done in a line or circle, giving the novice dancer the support of fellow dancers on either side. Historically, folk dancing, as I know it, began with the Greek hora about 3,000 years ago (as described by Homer).

The class introduces students to some of the multitude of ethnic dances that have evolved from the classical Greek Hora during the last 3,000 years. The Jewish dance, Hava Nagila, is a famous example of a folk dance that evolved from the Greek Hora!

Students who take the class receive all the wonderful music played in the class! And, soon, will receive videos of all our dances as well!

Introduction to Folk Dances

Marching Step Folk Dances

Marching step folk dances are done in a right, left, right, left regular rhythm. In the Greek tradition they begin with the right foot and compose 8 count patterns by combining two 4 count right, left, right, left steps.

Ve David Version 1

The music Ve David is a wonderful example of a marching step regular rhythm done in two 4 count steps combining to make 8 count patterns! This first version involves a 4 count step marching forward and a 4 count step marching backward creating the first 8 count pattern. Then a crossing over 4 count step moving left, beginning with the right foot crossing the left, moving in a right, left, right, left marching rhythm. That 4 count step is repeated to create our second 8 count pattern. The third 8 count pattern is composed of two 4 count turning steps with the right foot going around the left foot while the left remains in place! These three 8 count patterns are repeated by going forward 4 counts and backward 4 counts then two 4 count crossing steps moving right this time: right, left in front, right, left in front and two 4 count turning steps going around the right foot with the left. in front; 8 counts. Then the whole dance repeats ad infinitum!

Ve David Version 2

Version 2 is modelled after Version 1, and begins with the same 4 count marching steps forward and back. The second 8 count pattern also moves left but this time the right foot crosses both in front and in back of the left foot! This is universally called a grapevine step, and like its forerunners: crossing steps and turning steps, it is a 4 count step! Repeating the 4 count grapevine step creates an 8 count grapevine pattern moving left. The third pattern is a crossing step in place: right cross steps left, left in place, right in place, left cross steps right. For the second 4 counts: right in place, left in place, right cross steps left, left in place. This completes an 8 count cross steps in place pattern.

The second part of this dance is similar to the first: 4 count marching steps, forward and back, then two 4 count grapevine steps moving right: right in place, left crossing in front, right, left crossing behind. Then the third is an 8 count pattern of crossing steps in place: right in place, left crossing right, right in place, left in place, right crossing left, left in place, right in place, left crossing right. Repeat both parts ad infinitum.

This second version introduces the most elegantly flowing step to come from the Classical World or any world since, in my opinion! The grapevine step! The Jewish grapevine is particularly interesting in that it follows the Greek model by beginning with the right foot while maintaining the Jewish tradition of moving left.

Ve David Version 3

This third dance to the music Ve David is particularly important because it emphasizes using two different 4 count steps to create an 8 count pattern! Previously, the one 4 count step was repeated to make an 8 count pattern. The two 4 count steps in the second part of this third version are the grapevine step and the cross over step combining to create the 8 count pattern! One should recognize the other parts the the dance.

Ve David Version 4

Our fourth version, unlike its predecessors, does not introduce any new 4 count steps but attempts to do more than just express the mathematical structure of the music by arranging the 8 count patterns such that they also express the nonmathematical feeling the music conveys!

To accomplish that, the first part of the dance uses a marching step moving to the right and back, then, as in the other versions: forward and back. the second part is an 8 count cross step in place: right, left crossing right, right in place, left in place, right crossing left, left in place right in place, left crossing right. The third part continues the cross step: right in place, left in place, right cross left, left in place, followed by a 4 count turn step around the left. This third part repeats. Then the whole dance repeats, but the fourth time you need to repeat the third part a second time for it to feel right!

Lo Ahavti Dai

This is the our first dance that is presented in its original pre-COVID choreography except for dancing separately! It begins with two 4 count Jewish grapevine steps plus two Jewish grapevine steps in place. Then these two 8 count patterns are repeated.

The second part involves marching steps forward and back plus 4 count marching steps moving right with 4 count turning steps around right foot. Then, these two 8 count patterns are repeated, and that is the dance.

Yasmin

Our sixth dance uses the music, Yasmin. This dance features the grapevine step in its most common form, moving right in contrast to the Jewish version!

The first 8 count pattern is a combination of a marching step moving forward and a turning step 180 degrees around your left foot. This pattern is then repeated such that your new forward takes you back to where you began, repeat these two patterns then repeat the first pattern such that you end up in front. Follow this with two 4 count transition turning steps around your left foot. The second part begins with an 8 count pattern that combines a grapevine step with an in place grapevine, facing forward. This pattern is repeated continuing right. Return left to where you began, doing an 8 count cross steps. Then do two 4 count transition turning steps around your left foot and repeat this second part. Repeat the whole dance ad infinitum.

Please note! Patterns are 8 count, steps are 4 count. Any two steps can be combined to create a pattern! Also, any music you can tap your foot to, you can count, and hence, express using combinations of classical folk dance steps!

Mayim

The music for our seventh marching folk dance is Mayim, and like Lo Ahavti Dai, is taught as it was originally done with the exception of a final 8 count hopping and turning around your left foot.

It begins with two 8 count patterns of Jewish grapevine steps, followed by two 8 count patterns of marching steps moving forward and backward, singing "mayim, mayim, mayim, mayim" each time you go forward! The finale is marching right in 4 big steps to where you began then hopping double time in place on your right foot for 8 counts followed by hopping in place double time on your left foot while swinging your right foot to help you turn around your left foot for 8 counts! This doubly fast in place turn step is appropriately called a buzz step!
